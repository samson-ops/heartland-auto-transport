How Much Does Auto Transport Cost in 2026? Complete Pricing Guide

Everything you need to know about auto transport pricing — and how to avoid getting scammed by lowball quotes.

Published May 10, 2026 · Heartland Auto Transport

The Short Answer

Most auto transport shipments cost between $500 and $1,800 for standard vehicles. Short regional routes start around $400-$700. Cross-country shipments range $1,000-$1,800. Luxury, exotic, and oversized vehicles cost more. But the longer answer is more nuanced — and understanding the factors below will help you get accurate quotes and avoid getting scammed.

At Heartland Auto Transport, we believe in transparent pricing. The quote you accept is the price you pay. No hidden fees, no surprise increases mid-transit, no upcharges revealed at delivery. This guide explains exactly what drives auto transport costs in 2026 so you can budget accurately and recognize honest pricing when you see it.

The 6 Factors That Affect Auto Transport Cost

Every auto transport quote is built on six core variables. Understanding them helps you predict pricing and spot quotes that don't add up.

1. Distance. The biggest single factor. Longer trips cost more in absolute dollars but less per mile. A 500-mile shipment might cost $700 total ($1.40/mile). A 2,500-mile shipment might cost $1,500 total ($0.60/mile). Per-mile rates decrease with distance because carriers earn more on long-haul efficiency.

2. Vehicle Size and Weight. Full-size SUVs and pickup trucks cost 10-25% more than compact cars because they occupy more deck space and reduce total carrier capacity. A typical car carrier fits 9-10 sedans but only 6-7 full-size trucks. Oversized vehicles (lifted trucks, dually pickups, 3-row SUVs) may incur small surcharges.

3. Route Popularity. Major corridors are cheaper because more carriers compete on them. Alabama to Florida, Texas, Georgia, and the Carolinas are well-traveled and competitively priced. Remote pickups or deliveries off the interstate system cost more — carriers have to detour from their primary routes.

4. Time of Year. Summer is peak season for auto transport. May through August sees the highest demand (school transitions, military PCS moves, snowbird returns, summer relocations) and the highest prices. January and February are cheapest. Hurricane season can also affect Gulf Coast routes.

5. Pickup Flexibility. Carriers fill trailers based on routes and timing. Flexible pickup windows (5-7 days) get the best rates because the carrier can fit you in optimally. Demanding a specific date or same-day pickup costs more — sometimes significantly more for guaranteed dates.

6. Transport Type. Open transport (standard multi-car carriers) is the cheapest. Enclosed transport (covered trailers) costs 60-80% more but provides much better protection for classic, exotic, and luxury vehicles. Expedited shipping costs more for faster pickup and delivery.

How to Spot Scam Quotes

The auto transport industry has a serious problem with deceptive pricing. Some companies use a tactic called "lowball quoting" — they quote you an unrealistically low price to win your business, then jack up the price after you've waited days for pickup. Here's how to spot it.

Red flag 1: The quote is dramatically lower than competitors. If three companies quote $1,200 and one quotes $700, the $700 quote is almost certainly going to increase. Carriers won't accept loads that don't pay enough — and when no carrier accepts your shipment, the broker calls you to "renegotiate."

Red flag 2: They want a deposit before sharing carrier details. Legitimate brokers give you carrier information once you book — name, FMCSA authority, insurance certificate. Companies that won't share this are often shopping your shipment to whoever will take the lowball price.

Red flag 3: "Carrier rate increase" mid-transit. If a broker calls you after pickup to say the carrier needs more money to complete delivery, you're being scammed. The price was wrong at the start, and you're being held hostage with your vehicle as leverage.

Red flag 4: No transparent total at quote time. Legitimate quotes show the full all-in price. Quotes that say "starting at" or have fine print about additional fees are designed to be increased later.

How to protect yourself: get at least three quotes from established companies. If one is significantly lower, ask why. Request the carrier's name and FMCSA number before paying any deposit. Insist on all-in pricing in writing. And work with companies that have verifiable customer reviews on Google, Yelp, and BBB.

How to Save on Auto Transport

Some practical ways to reduce your auto transport cost without sacrificing service quality:

Be flexible with pickup dates. A 5-7 day pickup window gets significantly better pricing than a guaranteed specific date. If your timing isn't urgent, mention this when requesting a quote.

Ship in off-peak months. January and February are the cheapest months for auto transport. If you can shift your shipment to a slower season, you might save 15-25%.

Choose open transport unless you specifically need enclosed. Open transport is safe for the vast majority of vehicles — even brand-new luxury cars are delivered to dealerships on open carriers. Only choose enclosed if you have a classic, exotic, or high-value vehicle where the extra protection is worth the cost.

Ship from a major route, not a remote location. If you can drive your vehicle to a meeting location on a major interstate corridor (rather than having pickup at a remote address), you might save $100-$200.

Book early. Last-minute bookings cost more because carrier capacity is tighter. Book 2-3 weeks in advance for the best rates.

Consider terminal-to-terminal. Our terminal-to-terminal service costs less than door-to-door because the carrier doesn't have to detour for pickup and delivery. You drop off and pick up at freight terminals — less convenient but cheaper.

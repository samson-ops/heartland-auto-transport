Military PCS Auto Transport: Complete Guide for Service Members

Everything military families need to know about shipping vehicles during permanent change of station (PCS) moves.

Published April 25, 2026 · Heartland Auto Transport

Honoring Military Service

Heartland Auto Transport has shipped thousands of vehicles for active-duty service members, military families, and military retirees over the years. We understand the unique pressures of PCS moves: tight timelines, government bureaucracy, family logistics across state lines, and the financial squeeze of relocating on military pay. This guide covers everything you need to know about auto transport during a PCS — from what the military covers to how to save money on the parts you pay out of pocket.

We also extend a small thank-you to those who serve: active-duty military, veterans, and Gold Star families receive priority scheduling on their shipments. When you book with Heartland, mention your service and we'll prioritize your pickup window.

What Does the Military Cover?

The Department of Defense (DoD) provides some auto transport coverage for service members on PCS orders, but it has significant limitations.

OCONUS moves (Overseas): For PCS moves to or from OCONUS locations (Hawaii, Alaska, Guam, and overseas), the DoD typically ships one vehicle at government expense. The service member is responsible for additional vehicles.

CONUS moves (within the continental U.S.): For most CONUS PCS moves, the DoD does NOT provide auto transport. Service members are expected to either drive their vehicles to the new duty station or arrange (and pay for) their own shipping. The military reimburses a per-mile rate for personal vehicle transport (POV reimbursement), but this rate typically falls short of actual professional shipping costs.

Special circumstances: There are exceptions for medical hardship, multi-vehicle households where driving is impractical, and certain remote duty stations. Consult with your TMO (Transportation Management Office) for specifics.

What this means practically: most service members on CONUS PCS pay for their own auto transport. Smart planning and the right transport company makes a big difference.

Why Military Families Choose Professional Auto Transport

Driving a personally-owned vehicle (POV) cross-country during a PCS sounds simple but creates real problems for service members.

Time pressure: PCS timelines are tight. You have a report date at the new duty station. Driving from Alabama to California takes 4 days minimum. Add unexpected delays (weather, breakdowns) and you risk being late to your new assignment — a serious career issue.

Family logistics: Most military families have two vehicles. Driving both across the country means either splitting the family for the drive or making two separate trips. Neither is ideal during an already stressful move.

Wear and tear: A 2,500-mile cross-country drive adds significant wear to a vehicle. Fuel, oil changes, tire wear, and the risk of mechanical issues all add up to real costs.

Driver fatigue: Long-haul driving in unfamiliar territory increases accident risk. Service members are often dealing with move-related stress and family transitions on top of the driving itself.

Professional auto transport solves all of this. Your vehicle ships while you fly to the new duty station. You arrive rested, your car arrives in a few days, and your family stays together for the move.

PCS Auto Transport Tips

Book early. PCS season peaks in summer (May-August) when most permanent change of station moves happen. Book your shipment as soon as you have your orders — 4-6 weeks ahead is ideal. Last-minute summer bookings often face capacity constraints.

Coordinate with your TMO. Even though most CONUS auto transport is out-of-pocket, the Transportation Management Office can advise on POV reimbursement rates, OCONUS shipping, and any special program eligibility.

Keep all receipts. POV reimbursement requires documentation. Save fuel receipts, shipping receipts, and any other transportation costs for reimbursement claims.

Consider shipping both vehicles. If your family has two cars, shipping both is often cheaper per vehicle than driving one and shipping the other. Multi-vehicle shipments to the same destination get per-vehicle discounts.

Plan for the timing gap. Your vehicle takes 3-10 days to arrive at the new duty station. Plan for ground transportation at your destination — rental car, ride-share, or a fellow service member's vehicle — until your car arrives.

Ship valuables separately. Don't ship important personal items, military gear, or weapons in your vehicle. Auto transport doesn't cover personal items, and military regulations have specific rules about transporting issued equipment.

Communicate with your dispatcher. PCS timelines are tight. Make sure your dispatcher knows your report date and any inflexible deadlines. We work with military families regularly and understand the urgency.

Open vs. Enclosed Auto Transport: Which Should You Choose?

The two main auto transport options compared — cost differences, safety considerations, and which fits your vehicle.

Published May 5, 2026 · Heartland Auto Transport

The Quick Decision

If you're shipping a daily-driver sedan, SUV, pickup truck, or any vehicle worth less than $50,000, choose open auto transport. It's safe, affordable, and the same method used to deliver brand-new cars to dealerships nationwide.

If you're shipping a classic car, exotic supercar, luxury vehicle, antique, or anything worth $80,000+, choose enclosed auto transport. The extra protection from weather, road debris, and prying eyes is worth the premium for high-value vehicles.

That's the short answer. Let's get into the details.

What Is Open Auto Transport?

Open auto transport uses the multi-deck trailers you see every day on Alabama interstates — the ones hauling 7 to 10 vehicles at a time. Your car is loaded onto the trailer alongside other vehicles, secured with industry-standard wheel chocks and soft straps, and transported to its destination. The trailer is open to the elements: rain, sun, road dust, and the occasional bug.

Open transport accounts for roughly 95% of all auto shipments in the United States. Every new car you've ever bought was probably delivered to the dealership on an open carrier — including six-figure luxury vehicles. The auto industry trusts open transport because it works.

Open carriers are everywhere, which means more carrier availability, faster pickup windows, and lower per-vehicle costs. Your shipment shares capacity with other vehicles heading the same direction, which dramatically reduces the per-car expense.

What Is Enclosed Auto Transport?

Enclosed auto transport ships your vehicle inside a fully covered trailer — completely protected from weather, road debris, dust, and visibility from outside. Enclosed carriers fit 4-8 vehicles (versus 9-10 on open carriers), use hydraulic lift gates instead of ramps, employ soft polyester straps instead of chains, and typically operate with experienced specialty drivers familiar with high-value vehicles.

Enclosed transport is the standard for: classic and antique vehicles, exotic supercars (Ferrari, Lamborghini, McLaren), luxury vehicles over $80,000 (Mercedes S-Class, BMW 7 Series, Range Rover Autobiography), restored and concours-quality vehicles, vehicles with custom paint or aerodynamic packages, and any vehicle where the owner wants maximum protection.

Enclosed carriers are the same trucks that deliver new exotics from factories to high-end dealerships. The driver pool for enclosed transport is smaller and more specialized — these are professionals who've shipped Ferraris and Rolls-Royces hundreds of times.

Cost Comparison: Open vs. Enclosed

The biggest practical difference between open and enclosed transport is cost. Enclosed transport typically costs 60-80% more than open transport for the same route. For example, a Birmingham-to-Los Angeles shipment:
  • Open transport (standard sedan): $1,200-$1,500
  • Enclosed transport (same vehicle): $1,900-$2,400
  • Single-car enclosed transport (luxury/exotic): $3,500-$5,000+
The price difference reflects fundamental economics: enclosed carriers fit fewer vehicles, require more expensive equipment (hydraulic lift gates, climate control), and pay drivers who specialize in high-value vehicle transport. Higher service standards cost more to deliver. Is the premium worth it? For most daily drivers, no — open transport is safe and dramatically more affordable. For high-value vehicles where any damage could cost $5,000+ to repair, the enclosed premium is small insurance.

Safety: Is Open Transport Really Safe?

Yes — open transport is overwhelmingly safe. The auto manufacturing industry ships virtually every new car you've ever purchased via open carrier. Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Porsche, Audi, Cadillac, and every other manufacturer use open carriers to deliver inventory to dealerships. If open transport weren't safe, these companies wouldn't trust their multi-million-dollar inventory to it.

That said, open transport does expose your vehicle to minor risks: weather (rain, snow, road salt during winter, occasional hail), road debris (small stones kicked up by other traffic, bug splatter), dust accumulation, and visibility from outside (some thieves do scout interstate carriers for high-end vehicles).

Damage incidents in open transport are extremely rare — well under 1% of shipments — and when they occur, they're typically minor (a small stone chip, water spots from rain). The carrier's cargo insurance covers any actual damage. For high-value vehicles where even a minor stone chip means thousands in paint repair, enclosed is worth the premium.

Which Should You Choose?

Choose open transport if:
  • Your vehicle is a standard sedan, SUV, or pickup truck (any modern daily driver)
  • Your vehicle is worth less than $50,000
  • You're shipping for relocation, online purchase, snowbird, or military PCS
  • You want the most affordable option
  • You need faster pickup availability (more carriers means faster scheduling)
Choose enclosed transport if:
  • Your vehicle is a classic, antique, or vintage car
  • Your vehicle is an exotic supercar (Ferrari, Lamborghini, McLaren, etc.)
  • Your vehicle is a luxury car worth $80,000+ (Mercedes S-Class, BMW 7 Series, Bentley, etc.)
  • Your vehicle has custom paint, restoration work, or fragile aerodynamic features
  • You're shipping a restored show car to a concours or auction
  • Your vehicle has low ground clearance that needs lift-gate loading
  • You want maximum protection regardless of cost
Edge cases: for late-model luxury cars (BMW M-Series, AMG Mercedes, Audi RS, Porsche 911 daily drivers), it's a judgment call. Many owners ship them open without issue. Others prefer enclosed for the peace of mind. Talk to your dispatcher about your specific vehicle and use case.

How to Prepare Your Car for Shipping: 10 Essential Steps

A simple checklist to ensure your vehicle is ready when the carrier arrives — and protected from start to finish.

Published May 8, 2026 · Heartland Auto Transport

Why Preparation Matters

A little prep work before your auto transport pickup makes a huge difference in how smoothly the process goes. Properly preparing your vehicle helps document its true condition (protecting you from false damage claims), reduces the risk of issues during transit, prevents items from shifting and causing damage, and helps the driver complete pickup and delivery efficiently. The whole prep process takes about 30 minutes, but it pays off in peace of mind.

This checklist covers everything Heartland Auto Transport recommends before any shipment. Whether you're shipping a daily-driver Camry, a classic Mustang, or a brand-new Tesla, these 10 steps apply to virtually every vehicle.

Step 1: Wash Your Vehicle

This sounds counterintuitive — why wash a car that's about to spend a week on the road getting dirty again? Because at pickup, the driver inspects your vehicle for existing damage and documents it on the Bill of Lading. If your car is covered in road grime, small dings and scratches are easy to miss. If something gets damaged during transit, you'll have trouble proving it wasn't already there.

A clean vehicle creates a clear baseline. The driver can clearly see every existing scratch, ding, paint chip, and imperfection. This protects both you and the carrier. Wash the vehicle 24-48 hours before pickup. Don't worry about wax or detailing — basic cleanliness is what matters.

Step 2: Document the Condition with Photos

Take detailed photos of your vehicle from every angle before pickup. Get the front, rear, both sides, roof, hood, trunk, all four wheels, and any pre-existing damage close-up. Include the dashboard showing odometer reading. Timestamp the photos if your camera supports it (most smartphones do automatically).

These photos are your insurance policy. If anything happens during transit, the timestamped photos prove the vehicle's exact condition at the moment of pickup. Combined with the Bill of Lading documentation, you have an ironclad record. Store the photos in cloud backup (Google Photos, iCloud) so they're accessible even if your phone is lost or damaged.

Step 3: Remove Personal Items (Mostly)

Most carriers allow up to 100 pounds of personal items in the trunk below the window line. However, personal items are NOT covered by the carrier's cargo insurance. If something gets stolen or damaged in transit, you're out of luck. So follow this rule: ship only what you can afford to lose.

Never leave: irreplaceable items, electronics (laptops, cameras, gaming systems), important documents (passports, social security cards), jewelry, cash, firearms, ammunition, hazardous materials, perishables, or anything illegal. Items above the window line are typically prohibited because they can shift during transit and cause interior damage. Empty the visible interior — center console, glove box, door pockets, seat surfaces.

Step 4: Reduce Gas to a Quarter Tank

A full gas tank adds 100+ pounds of unnecessary weight to your vehicle, which affects the carrier's total weight capacity and fuel costs. Most carriers ask for vehicles to arrive with about a quarter tank of fuel. Don't drain it completely — the driver needs to be able to move the vehicle on and off the trailer at pickup, transit checkpoints, and delivery.

A quarter tank is enough for loading and unloading without adding meaningful weight. If your vehicle is electric, charge to about 50% state of charge for similar reasons (see our EV shipping page for EV-specific prep).

Step 5: Disable Aftermarket Alarms

Aftermarket car alarms can be triggered by the bumps and vibrations of carrier transport, draining your battery during the trip. Disable any aftermarket alarm before pickup. Factory alarms are usually fine — they're integrated with the vehicle's systems and don't trigger from normal transport motion.

If your alarm can't be disabled, tell the driver. They may know specific procedures for handling it, or recommend pulling the alarm fuse. A dead battery at delivery is a common preventable issue.

Step 6: Fold Mirrors In and Retract Antenna

Side mirrors and antennas are the most exposed parts of your vehicle on a multi-car carrier. Fold mirrors in if they have that feature (most modern vehicles do, either manually or via a button). Retract any antenna if possible — most modern antennas are short stubs that don't need to retract, but older vehicles with telescoping antennas should be retracted.

For convertibles, ensure the soft top is fully up and latched. For vehicles with sunroofs, make sure the sunroof is fully closed. Any open or partially-open feature is an invitation for water damage during transit.

Step 7: Check for Leaks and Mechanical Issues

Carriers don't accept vehicles that leak significantly (fluid leaks can damage other vehicles below on the carrier). Check for: oil leaks under the vehicle, coolant leaks (look for green or pink puddles), transmission fluid leaks (red), brake fluid leaks (clear to yellow). If your vehicle has minor leaks, tell the dispatcher upfront — there are workarounds, but surprise leaks at pickup cause delays.

Also note any mechanical issues: doesn't start reliably, won't shift gears, tires won't hold air, steering wheel locks, brakes don't work, parking brake doesn't release. Non-running vehicles can be transported but require winch-equipped carriers and an upfront notice — never surprise a driver with a non-running vehicle at pickup.

Step 8: Provide Keys (and a Backup)

The driver needs a working key or fob at pickup to load the vehicle. Provide one set of keys to the driver. If you have a backup set, keep it with you — never give your only set of keys to the carrier. After delivery, the driver returns the keys to you (or the designated recipient at the delivery address).

For vehicles with electronic key fobs, ensure the fob battery is fresh. A dead fob at pickup causes major delays. For vehicles with valet keys or limited-function keys, provide the master key so the driver can fully operate the vehicle if needed.

Step 9: Document Existing Damage

Even with photos, walking through existing damage with the driver at pickup is crucial. Point out: every scratch, ding, paint chip, dent, cracked windshield, broken trim, missing parts, or unusual wear. The driver documents all of this on the Bill of Lading — the legal document recording your vehicle's condition.

Don't be embarrassed about pointing out existing damage. The whole purpose of the Bill of Lading is to establish a baseline. If you skip this step, any post-transit damage claim is much harder to prove. Spend an extra 5 minutes walking around the vehicle with the driver. It's the most important 5 minutes of the entire process.

Step 10: Confirm Pickup Details

Before pickup day, confirm the details with your dispatcher: the date and time window, the pickup address (and whether the driver can legally access it), who will be present (yourself or a designated representative 18+ with photo ID), payment method for the remaining balance, and contact phone numbers for both you and the driver. Most issues at pickup come from communication gaps that 5 minutes of confirmation would have prevented.

If your address has access restrictions (gated community, low overhead clearance, narrow streets, no truck access), tell the dispatcher in advance. We'll coordinate a nearby meeting location like a large parking lot or rest area. Showing up to an inaccessible address and discovering it on pickup day is the worst-case scenario.

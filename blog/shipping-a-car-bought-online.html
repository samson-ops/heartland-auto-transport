Shipping a Car You Bought Online: Carvana, Vroom & eBay Guide

Step-by-step guide to shipping a vehicle you purchased remotely — including timing, coordination, and delivery inspection.

Published April 28, 2026 · Heartland Auto Transport

The Rise of Online Car Buying

Buying cars online has exploded over the past decade. Carvana, Vroom, CarMax online, eBay Motors, Cars.com, AutoTrader online listings, dealer websites, and private seller marketplaces like Facebook Marketplace have made it possible to buy a vehicle from across the country without ever seeing it in person. The vehicles are inspected, photographed, and shipped — all without you setting foot in a dealership.

The one piece online car buying companies often handle poorly is the shipping itself. Carvana and Vroom offer in-house shipping, but it's typically slower and more expensive than third-party auto transport. Many savvy buyers arrange their own shipping with companies like Heartland Auto Transport, saving hundreds of dollars and getting faster delivery.

This guide walks through the entire process of shipping a car you bought online — from coordinating with the seller to inspecting the vehicle at delivery.

Common Online Car Purchase Scenarios

We handle a wide range of online purchase shipments to Alabama buyers (and from Alabama sellers). The most common scenarios:

Carvana purchases: Carvana operates vehicle hubs across the country. When you buy from Carvana, the vehicle ships from one of these hubs to your local hub or directly to you. Many Alabama buyers use Heartland to ship from out-of-state Carvana hubs (Atlanta, Charlotte, Phoenix, Dallas, etc.) directly to their Alabama address rather than using Carvana's slow in-house delivery.

Vroom purchases: Similar to Carvana — Vroom ships from regional facilities. Their in-house delivery is notoriously slow. Many buyers arrange independent shipping for faster delivery.

eBay Motors: When you buy a car on eBay, the seller is responsible for getting it to you — or you arrange shipping. eBay Motors transactions often involve coordination with private sellers across the country, which is exactly what we handle every day.

Dealer-to-dealer or dealer-to-buyer: Buying from an out-of-state dealership? They'll typically need shipping arranged. We coordinate with the selling dealer directly.

Auction purchases: Manheim, Copart, IAA, and other dealer/wholesale auctions. We pick up directly from the auction yard.

Private seller purchases: Found a car on Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, or a classic car forum? We coordinate with the seller and pick up at their location.

Step-by-Step: Shipping Your Online Purchase

Step 1: Complete the purchase first. Pay for the vehicle and get the title transferred or in transit before arranging shipping. The shipping company should not be involved in payment — never wire money to a shipping company for the vehicle purchase itself.

Step 2: Get the seller's pickup address and contact info. Confirm exactly where the vehicle is and who will release it. For Carvana, this is a specific Carvana hub. For private sellers, it's their home or storage location. For dealers, it's the dealer lot.

Step 3: Get a quote from us. Provide the pickup address, your delivery address, vehicle year/make/model, and your timing flexibility. We'll quote the shipment within minutes.

Step 4: Book the shipment. Once you accept the quote, we coordinate directly with the seller for pickup. You don't need to be present at pickup — we handle seller coordination.

Step 5: Driver picks up the vehicle. The driver inspects the vehicle with the seller, signs the Bill of Lading documenting condition, and transports it to you.

Step 6: Inspect at delivery (this is the most important step — see below).

Step 7: Sign the Bill of Lading, take possession, and enjoy your new vehicle.

What to Inspect at Delivery (Critical)

When you buy a car online, you haven't seen it in person before delivery. The delivery moment is your first chance to confirm the vehicle matches what you bought. Spend at least 30 minutes inspecting before you sign the Bill of Lading.

Exterior: Walk around the entire vehicle. Check every panel for damage that wasn't disclosed. Compare to the seller's photos. Look for: undisclosed scratches, dings, dents, paint variations (different shades on different panels suggests previous repair), rust, replaced glass, or aftermarket trim.

Interior: Check seat condition, dashboard for cracks, all controls and electronics, all interior lights, air conditioning and heat (test both), all power features (windows, locks, seats), and odometer reading (compare to the listing).

Mechanical: Start the engine and listen for unusual sounds. Check fluid levels (oil, coolant). Verify all warning lights extinguish properly. Test the transmission by shifting through gears in a safe area. Test the brakes. Check tire condition and tread depth.

Compare to the listing: Have the original listing photos and description on your phone. Compare every claim — features, options, color, mileage — to what you actually received.

Document everything before signing. The Bill of Lading is signed when you take possession. Once you sign and the carrier leaves, any disputes are with the seller, not the carrier. If something is wrong — major damage, missing features, different mileage — note it on the Bill of Lading before signing, take photos, and contact us immediately.

Common Issues and How to Handle Them

The vehicle has undisclosed damage: If you find damage that wasn't disclosed in the listing, document it with photos, note it on the Bill of Lading, and contact the seller. For Carvana and Vroom, their return policies typically cover this (7-day satisfaction guarantees). For private sellers and auctions, disputes are usually handled through the marketplace (eBay buyer protection, etc.).

The mileage is higher than listed: Document it on the Bill of Lading. Mileage discrepancies are serious issues — sometimes innocent (the seller drove more after listing), sometimes fraudulent.

The vehicle doesn't start at delivery: If the vehicle was running when picked up but won't start at delivery, this is a transit issue. Most often it's a dead battery from sitting on the carrier — easy fix. Sometimes it's something more serious. Don't sign the Bill of Lading without noting the issue. We coordinate with both the carrier and seller to resolve.

Damage occurred during transit: If damage is clearly new (matches what the carrier carries — like horizontal scratches across a roof, or hood dings from cargo above), document it carefully on the Bill of Lading. The carrier's cargo insurance covers transit damage. Don't refuse the vehicle — sign with damage noted, take photos, and let us coordinate the claim.
